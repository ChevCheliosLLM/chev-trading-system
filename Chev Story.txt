================================================================================
THE CHEV STORY
How a hitman, a bookworm, a bouncer, a scribe, an honest man, a scout, and a
tireless night-shift worker all ended up running one trading desk together.
================================================================================

A note before we start: everything in this story is true to how the system
actually works. The characters and personalities are made up to make it easier
to hold in your head — but what each of them DOES, what they check, what they
argue about, and what actually went wrong a few hours ago tonight, all really
happened, exactly as described. Nothing here is exaggerated for drama. The real
story already had enough drama in it.


PART ONE — MEET THE CREW
================================================================================

DEXTER — the one who never sleeps

Dexter is the night-shift worker. He doesn't sleep, doesn't take breaks, and
doesn't have opinions of his own. His whole job is to watch — dozens of pairs,
crypto and forex and stocks, on multiple timeframes, all the time, every few
seconds. He's counting things: has price touched this support level four times
before? Is there a Fibonacci retracement sitting here? Is the RSI diverging?
Did a Bollinger Band just burst?

Dexter isn't smart in the way you'd call a person smart. He's thorough. He adds
up points for every technical thing he notices — a support test is worth this
many points, a confirmed divergence is worth that many — and when the total
crosses a line, he stops watching and starts talking. He walks over to Chev's
desk with a folder full of everything he noticed and says, essentially, "I
think there's something here — take a look."

Dexter also runs the whole building, in a sense. He's the one who fetches the
candle data in the first place, wakes the other characters in this story up
when they're needed, writes down what happened afterward, and keeps the lights
on 24 hours a day. Everyone else in this story only exists because Dexter
calls on them.

  DEXTER'S RULEBOOK, IN ACTUAL NUMBERS:
  - The line he has to cross before he calls Chev over: 10 points for crypto,
    8 for forex, 8 for stocks — that's the normal bar. During the current
    training season (EXPLORATION_MODE), it's temporarily lower: 7 for
    crypto, 6 for forex, 6 for stocks.
  - How far price is allowed to be from the actual level he's excited about
    before he stops caring: 0.75% normally, widened to 1.5% during the
    training season.
  - Counter-trend setups (betting against the higher-timeframe trend) get
    taxed hard: the score needed is 1.5× the normal bar — so during the
    training season that's roughly 10.5 for crypto instead of 7 — plus a
    confirmed RSI divergence and a clear break-of-structure are both
    required, no exceptions.


CHEV CHELIOS — the one who actually decides

Chev is the only one in the building with a personality, and it's a loud one —
built, deliberately, on the character from the Crank movies: a hitman who was
poisoned and told his heart will stop if he ever slows down. In this building
that backstory isn't just flavor text, it's a working metaphor for how Chev is
supposed to think: hesitation is dangerous, a weak setup with nothing backing
it up is dangerous, and he needs "voltage" — real confluence, real structure —
or the whole thing falls apart.

Dexter hands Chev a folder. Everyone else in this story (you'll meet them in a
moment) has already added their own notes to that folder before it reaches
him. Chev's job is to read all of it, decide whether there's a real trade
here, and if so, say exactly where he'd get in, where he's wrong, and where
he's taking profit. If there isn't a real trade, he says so, and — this
matters a lot, you'll see why later — he's supposed to say exactly WHY, citing
something real from the chart, not a feeling.

Chev only ever gets ONE reply. He can't ask Dexter follow-up questions in the
middle of a live decision — everything he needs has to already be in the
folder. He reads it, and he answers: POST or SKIP.

Chev also has a second, much calmer job that has nothing to do with Dexter.
Kev — the person who built all of this — talks to Chev directly sometimes,
just to ask what he thinks about a pair. For that conversation, Chev has his
own personal sketchbook (a separate little toolkit that fetches candles and
draws charts) that he uses only when Kev is in the room asking questions
directly. It's a simpler version of what you're about to meet next — good
enough for a casual conversation, not wired into the live decision-making at
all.

  CHEV'S RULEBOOK, IN ACTUAL NUMBERS:
  - His own hard skip conditions, always in force outside a training
    season: an "auction" older than 85% of its typical lifespan is too
    ripe to enter fresh; participation below 25 means the market's too
    dead for any setup to be valid; a direction score fighting his thesis
    by more than 50 points means the market is telling him he's wrong;
    a TRANSITIONING market state means no playbook applies at all, full
    stop. During a training season, these four stop being automatic vetoes
    on Dexter's escalations specifically and become sizing/conviction
    inputs instead — Kev's own chat conversations with Chev are never
    affected either way.
  - Suggested risk per trade, by letter grade: roughly 0.75% for a B,
    1.5% for an A, 2.5% for an A+ — always graded against the FULL normal
    bar, never the lowered training-season one, which is the actual seatbelt
    of the whole training season: a lowered entry bar never means bigger
    size, only smaller.
  - Leverage ceilings: up to 10× for crypto scalps/day trades (5× for
    swings), up to 5× for forex/stock scalps/day trades (2× for swings).
  - As of tonight: once a real Opportunity idea exists at all, he sizes
    continuously off its 0-100 Conviction number rather than a letter
    grade — roughly, 70+ supports full size, 40-70 means take it smaller,
    and below 40 still means take it at minimum size, never an automatic
    walk-away just because conviction is merely moderate rather than high.


ELLIOT — the quiet one who only looks at shape

Elliot doesn't care about news, doesn't care about indicators like RSI, and
barely even looks at the same things Dexter looks at. Elliot has one obsession:
the actual SHAPE the price is drawing. Give him a stretch of candles and he'll
tell you, purely from geometry, what's going on — and he does it in a strict,
almost ritualistic order, always the same six steps:

  1. SWING — first, find every meaningful peak and valley in the price.
  2. LEG — then connect those peaks and valleys into "legs," and grade each
     leg: was that an aggressive, decisive move, or a tired, corrective one?
  3. STATE — step back and ask: overall, right now, who's actually in
     control here? He puts a number on it — participation (is anyone
     showing up to trade this at all) and direction (which way is the crowd
     leaning) — because a chart with no one trading it is not a chart worth
     acting on, no matter how pretty it looks.
  4. GEOMETRY — draw a line along the recent highs, another along the recent
     lows, and ask: are they squeezing together (a triangle), running
     parallel (a channel), or spreading apart? This is where Elliot spends
     most of his time — literally fitting straight lines through the recent
     swing points and measuring how well they actually fit.
  5. AUCTION — figure out the "trading range" everyone's currently arguing
     over — where's the most-traded price, where's the top and bottom of
     the accepted zone, and how OLD is this range (a very old, "overripe"
     range is dangerous — it's about to break one way or the other).
  6. HYPOTHESIS — only now, with all five of the above in hand, does Elliot
     allow himself a guess: "this looks like a bull flag," or "this looks
     like an ascending triangle." He's required to name what's FOR that
     idea and what's AGAINST it, in the same breath — he's not allowed to
     just tell you the good news.

At the very end, Elliot hands off to one more step — the OPPORTUNITY — which
takes his single best-guess pattern and turns it into one concrete, actionable
idea: here's where you'd get in, here's where you'd be proven wrong, here's
the target, and here's a plain 0-to-100 conviction number for how much he
actually believes it.

Here's the important thing to understand about Elliot, because it's the whole
first half of tonight's story: Elliot's conviction number and Dexter's point
total are computed completely independently of each other, by two different
sets of eyes looking at two different things. They usually agree. Sometimes
they don't. And for a while tonight, nobody had noticed that they'd stopped
listening to each other at all.

  ELLIOT'S RULEBOOK, IN ACTUAL NUMBERS:
  - Old letter grades (now cosmetic only, no longer a gate): A+ needed 70%+
    confidence AND a 2.0:1 structural reward-to-risk; A needed 55%+ and
    1.5:1; B needed 40%+ and 1.0:1; anything less was a C.
  - His confidence used to be "whichever of four measurements is worst, full
    stop" — now: if any single measurement falls below a rock-bottom 15%
    (a genuine red flag, like a dead market), that alone still disqualifies
    it. Otherwise, the four measurements are blended — the pattern actually
    matching its own definition counts for 40% of the final number, and the
    other three (how clean the lines are, how clear the market's mood is,
    how well-formed the trading range is) share the remaining 60% evenly.
  - Wick/fakeout forgiveness: any swing point within 0.3× the asset's own
    average daily wiggle (ATR) of his fitted line now counts as a perfect
    touch, not a broken pattern — capped so that forgiveness can never
    swallow more than 30% of however much room is actually left in a
    narrowing shape, which protects a triangle from getting an unearned
    pass right near its tip.


GEORGE — the bouncer with a calculator

If Chev says "yes, take it," the trade doesn't happen automatically. It goes
through George first. George doesn't care about chart patterns, doesn't care
about the story, doesn't care if Chev is excited about it. George cares about
one thing: money, and whether this specific trade, with these specific
numbers, is actually a sound bet.

George runs down a checklist, in order, and any one failure stops the trade
cold:

  - Is the stop-loss on the correct side of the entry at all? (You'd be
    surprised how often this needs checking.)
  - Is the stop far enough away, given how much this asset actually moves
    day to day, that ordinary noise won't take you out by accident?
  - Are the trading costs — fees, slippage — going to eat an unreasonable
    chunk of the risk, on a stop this tight?
  - Given the ACTUAL history of how well this kind of setup performs, is the
    potential reward big enough to be worth it? (This is the "Net R:R"
    check, and it's the star of tonight's story — more in Part Three.)
  - Is the position size sane for the account? Would leverage push this
    trade dangerously close to a liquidation?
  - How much is ALREADY at risk across every other open trade — is this new
    one about to push the whole portfolio too hot?
  - Are there already too many trades open in the same direction on
    correlated assets (several coins that all move together, say)?
  - Is there already the maximum number of this TYPE of trade (day trades,
    swings) open at once?
  - Is the price data even fresh, or has it gone stale?

Only if every single one of these passes does George actually let the trade
through — and even then, he's the one who does the final math on position
size, adjusting Chev's numbers if leverage needs capping.

George has one important character trait worth knowing: he treats a trade's
weakest reason as the whole verdict. If a setup has five great things going
for it and one mediocre one, George grades the WHOLE trade by the mediocre
one. It's meant to protect against being fooled by a pile of decent-but-not-
great evidence — but as you'll see, that same instinct is exactly what got
George into trouble tonight.

  GEORGE'S RULEBOOK, IN ACTUAL NUMBERS:
  - Stop-distance floor (as a multiple of the asset's own ATR): normally
    1.0× for a scalp, 1.2× for a day trade, 1.5× for a swing. During the
    training season, those relax to 0.6× / 0.7× / 1.0×.
  - Cost ceiling (how much of your risk trading costs are allowed to eat):
    normally 20% / 25% / 30% for scalp / day / swing. Training season:
    35% / 40% / 45%.
  - The flat reward-to-risk floor, before any history is factored in:
    normally 2.0:1 / 1.8:1 / 2.5:1. Training season: 1.3:1 / 1.2:1 / 1.6:1.
    There's also a hard absolute minimum that never lets go even when the
    smart version below is being generous: 1.1:1 / 1.1:1 / 1.3:1 normally,
    0.8:1 / 0.8:1 / 1.0:1 during the training season.
  - The SMART version of that reward-to-risk check — the one this whole
    story is really about — only kicks in once a tag has at least 10 closed
    trades on record. Below that, it just uses the flat floor above. Once
    it does kick in: take the worst-performing tag on the trade, take its
    win rate, work out the breakeven ratio that win rate implies, and then
    add a 15% safety cushion on top of that breakeven number. That's the
    bar the trade has to clear.
  - AS OF TONIGHT: that win-rate number itself no longer means "every trade
    this tag has ever been part of, forever." It now means "this tag's most
    recent 35 trades, whenever they happened" — so a good tag that trades
    rarely is never punished just for being infrequent, and old results
    eventually age out on their own as new ones come in. On top of that, a
    thin sample gets gently pulled toward the overall average — with a
    strength equivalent to 10 imaginary "average" trades mixed in — so a
    tag's very first handful of results can't swing its grade to an extreme
    all by themselves the way they used to.
  - Portfolio heat cap (total risk allowed across every open trade at once):
    8% normally, 25% during the training season.
  - Correlation cap (same-direction trades on assets that move together):
    2 normally, 3 during the training season.
  - Concurrency cap (how many day trades / swings can be open at the same
    time): 1 day / 2 swing normally, 2 day / 3 swing during the training
    season.


THE EXAMINER — the one who never forgets, and never lets go

Here's a character most people don't even know is in the building, because he
never talks to Chev directly. The Examiner watches every single setup Dexter
ever raises — not just the ones Chev takes, ALL of them, including every
single skip — and quietly keeps a shadow file on each one.

For every setup, the Examiner asks a simple question and waits for the answer,
however long it takes: if someone HAD taken this trade, right now, at this
price, would it have hit the target first, hit the stop first, or just run out
the clock without doing either? This is called "triple-barrier" tracking —
three doors, and the Examiner waits to see which one price walks through.

Once enough of these shadow records resolve, the Examiner can answer a much
bigger question: out of every trade that's ever been tagged "near a support
level," how many actually would have won? Out of every trade tagged "at a
golden pocket Fibonacci level," how many actually won? This becomes the tag-
by-tag win-rate record that both Kev's own dashboard AND George both lean on.

The Examiner's whole value is that he remembers EVERYTHING, forever, and never
edits the past. Which sounds like a virtue right up until it becomes a
problem — and it did, tonight. More in Part Three.

  THE EXAMINER'S RULEBOOK, IN ACTUAL NUMBERS:
  - A setup only counts as "touched" once price comes within 0.1% of the
    entry, or a quarter of an ATR, whichever is bigger.
  - How long he'll wait before calling it a timeout instead of a win or
    loss: 2 hours for a scalp, 6 hours for a day trade, 48 hours for a
    swing — the same clock Chev's own trade-type expiries run on.
  - He won't report a tag's win rate as trustworthy until it has at least
    30 "effective" resolved samples, and won't suggest adjusting anything's
    importance based on a tag until it has at least 50.


MARC — the honest one

Marc's entire job is refusing to let anyone lie to themselves about outcomes,
including by accident. When a trade closes, it would be easy to just assume
the exit price was clean — but Marc insists on checking against the ACTUAL
candle-by-candle price action to see exactly when and where the stop or
target was really touched, not just approximately. He adds in REAL trading
costs on top, so nobody's quietly celebrating a win that would have actually
been a loss after fees and slippage.

Marc also holds the one emergency brake in the whole building: if the account
loses more than three times its normal risk unit in a single day, Marc calls
a halt — no new trades for the rest of the day, full stop, no exceptions,
no arguing. Bad days happen; the point is making sure a bad day never turns
into a catastrophic one.

  MARC'S RULEBOOK, IN ACTUAL NUMBERS:
  - The emergency brake trips at -3.0R in a single day, exactly.
  - He checks real exit prices on 1-minute candles for crypto, 15-minute
    candles for forex and stocks — fine enough to catch the truth without
    drowning in noise.


THE SCOUT — the crypto specialist

The Scout only cares about one corner of the market: crypto derivatives.
Specifically, he watches funding rates (are traders paying a premium to stay
long or short right now — a sign of a crowded, possibly overextended trade)
and open interest (is money actually flowing into new positions, or is this
move happening on stale bets). He hands his notes to Dexter just like
everyone else, tagged so they land in the right bucket.

  THE SCOUT'S RULEBOOK, IN ACTUAL NUMBERS:
  - Funding rate worth mentioning: 0.05% per 8 hours. Funding rate worth
    calling "extreme," crowded-trade territory: 0.10%.
  - Open interest move worth caring about: a 2% swing over 6 hours,
    alongside at least a 1% price move in the same window.


JANE — the other trader in the building

Jane isn't part of Dexter's automated scanning at all — she's a second,
completely independent trader who brings her OWN trade ideas to the desk,
simpler ones, without the full confluence analysis. Chev's job with Jane is
different: he's not originating the idea, he's grading someone else's. Every
so often — every 20 or so trades between the two of them — Chev sits down and
reviews both his own track record and Jane's side by side, looking for
patterns in what's actually working, and updates his own notes accordingly.


THE CONTROL ROOM — where Kev actually watches all of this

Everything above happens mostly out of sight. The Control Room is the website
Kev actually looks at, and it has a few distinct areas:

  - THE WATCHLIST, where every pair being tracked lives, with a little star
    you can tap on any of them.
  - THE RADAR, a lookout tower that only shows the pairs you've starred. From
    here you can also directly ask Chev, on demand, "if you HAD to take this
    right now, where would you enter?" — even for a setup he'd otherwise
    skip live. That answer is clearly marked as off-the-record: it never
    gets written to the real trade log, it's purely for Kev's own curiosity.
  - THE STRATEGY DASHBOARD, the review room — a live feed of every decision
    Chev and the gates have made recently, a leaderboard of which tags
    actually win, and the full raw log if you want to dig in yourself.
  - CHEV'S CORNER, where any live idea Chev is actually excited about sits
    waiting for Kev to look at, with the reasoning attached.


PART TWO — HOW ONE TRADE ACTUALLY HAPPENS, START TO FINISH
================================================================================

Let's walk one realistic setup through the whole building, the way it would
actually happen.

It's 2 in the morning. Dexter is watching ETHUSDT on the 1-hour chart, same as
always, and he notices price sitting right at a support level that's held
three times before, right at a Fibonacci retracement zone, with the RSI
quietly diverging. He adds up his points. It crosses his threshold. Folder
time.

Before he even calls Chev, Dexter has already asked Elliot to take a look at
the same chart completely independently. Elliot goes through his six steps —
swing, leg, state, geometry, auction, hypothesis — and comes back with: "I
see a bull channel forming, confidence is decent, here's where I'd put the
entry zone, here's the invalidation, here's a conviction score out of 100."
The Scout chimes in too, if it's a crypto pair, with a note about funding
rates.

Dexter bundles all of this — his own point total, Elliot's structural read,
the Scout's derivatives note, the account's current open positions, and
Kev's own trading notes for this asset class — into one message, and hands
it to Chev.

Chev reads all of it. He's not allowed to just glance at Dexter's score and
nod — he has to independently reason through it: what's the higher-timeframe
trend doing, does the RSI actually confirm, where's the real structural stop,
is the reward actually worth the risk. If he likes it, he writes: POST, here's
my entry, here's my stop, here's my target, here's why, tagging every
confluence he actually used. If he doesn't like it, he writes SKIP — and (as
of tonight, more on this later) he has to say exactly which real, checkable
thing was missing, not just a vague feeling.

Say Chev likes it and posts. The idea doesn't go live yet — it goes straight
to George. George runs his whole checklist: stop on the right side, wide
enough for this asset's normal noise, costs not eating the reward, the reward
actually big enough given this tag's real track record, position size sane,
portfolio not overheating, no correlation pile-up, no concurrency problem.
Only if ALL of that clears does George finalize the position size and the
trade actually goes live.

Whether Chev posted or skipped, the Examiner has already quietly opened a
shadow file on this exact setup, and will keep watching price until it either
hits target, hits stop, or the clock runs out — regardless of what Chev
decided. That shadow record is what eventually updates the tag win-rate
numbers everyone else relies on later.

If the trade does go live and eventually closes, Marc checks the real candle
data to confirm exactly how and when it closed, applies real costs, and logs
the honest result. If it's been a rough day and the account's down three
units of risk, Marc's circuit breaker kicks in and nothing new gets opened
until tomorrow, no matter how good the next setup looks.

That's the whole loop. It happens dozens of times a day, mostly resulting in
a SKIP, because most setups genuinely aren't good enough — that part is
completely normal and by design.


PART THREE — THE NIGHT NOBODY TRADED
================================================================================

This part is the most recent chapter, and it's worth telling properly, because
it's the clearest possible demonstration of how all these characters actually
interact — including how they can quietly work against each other without
anyone noticing for a while.

It started with a simple, uncomfortable observation from Kev: Chev hadn't
posted a single trade in over 53 hours. Not "here's one weak trade," not
"here's one small win" — nothing. Zero.

The first instinct was to suspect George — maybe the bouncer was being
overzealous, rejecting things Chev actually wanted to take. So the decision
log was pulled apart and counted. The result was almost the opposite of the
suspicion: over 92% of the "no trade" outcomes were Chev's OWN skip decisions,
not George rejecting something Chev had approved. Whatever the problem was, it
was upstream of George. Chev himself kept saying no.

And when his actual reasons were read closely, a pattern jumped out
immediately: phrases like "confluence quality below threshold despite strong
indicators." Technically true-sounding. Actually meaningless — it doesn't
point at anything specific on the chart at all.

Digging further explained WHY Chev kept talking like that. A while back, Kev
had deliberately started a temporary, lower-bar "training season" — lowering
Dexter's own point threshold on purpose, specifically so more setups would
reach Chev and start generating fresh data. Dexter's bar really had come down.
But it turned out Elliot's conviction grading — his A+/A/B/C letter system —
had never been told about the training season at all. Nobody had wired the
two together. So more setups were reaching Chev past Dexter's now-lower bar,
but Elliot was still grading every single one of them by his old, unchanged,
much stricter yardstick — and Chev's own instructions told him to treat a
mediocre grade from Elliot as a reason to demand extra proof, or just walk
away. Two bouncers at two different doors, and only one of them had been told
the rules had changed.

On top of that, Elliot's grading itself turned out to be needlessly harsh in
its own right: his geometry check punished every ordinary wick and false
breakout as if it were proof the whole pattern was broken, with zero
tolerance for normal market noise. And his conviction math was built so that
one weak ingredient — even a single slightly-noisy measurement — could drag
an otherwise excellent read all the way down, no matter how strong everything
else looked.

So that got fixed, three ways at once: Elliot learned to forgive an ordinary
amount of wick noise instead of treating it as a broken chart. His scoring
softened from "one bad ingredient ruins everything" to "one truly
catastrophic ingredient still disqualifies you, but otherwise your average
matters." And most importantly, his letter grade stopped being a gate at all
— it became a cosmetic label, while a plain, continuous conviction number
became what actually drives how much size a trade gets. Nobody's allowed to
just say "well it's only a B, skip it" anymore.

That felt like real progress — and it was — but there was still a nagging
follow-up question: was Chev's reasoning actually improving, or just
sounding a little different? And that's when a second, embarrassing gap
turned up: for every SKIP, the ONE-SENTENCE quick reason was being kept, but
the fuller explanation Chev was writing right alongside it — the part that
actually said something specific — was being thrown away before it ever
reached the log. Nobody downstream ever saw it. It was like asking someone to
explain themselves, having them give a genuinely detailed answer, and then
only writing down the first three words.

That got fixed too: the fuller reasoning now gets kept, for both takes and
skips, and there's a simple, honest built-in check — does this explanation
actually contain a real number or price level at all, or is it still just a
feeling with no evidence attached. Vague answers now get flagged automatically
instead of hiding in plain sight.

And it was THAT fix — being able to finally read Chev's full, real reasoning
side by side across a whole night — that surfaced the actual, final answer.
Reading closely, a huge number of the skips all pointed at the exact same
place: a required reward-to-risk ratio that was absurdly high, sometimes
close to four times the risk, for perfectly ordinary day trades.

That number was coming from George — specifically, from the part of his
checklist that adjusts how much reward you need based on how well this KIND
of setup has actually performed historically, according to the Examiner's
records. And when those records were pulled directly, the problem was
obvious: the most commonly used tags — the ones behind a huge share of every
setup Chev ever looks at — had a genuinely poor track record on paper. Not a
poor track record NOW. A poor track record from BEFORE any of tonight's other
fixes even existed, sitting frozen in the Examiner's memory, exactly the way
he'd recorded it and never once revisited.

Which meant George was quietly judging every brand-new trade against a
version of the operation that didn't really exist anymore — and the only way
those old numbers could ever improve was by new trades actually closing and
giving the Examiner fresh evidence. But the old numbers themselves were
exactly what was stopping new trades from happening at all. A locked door
that could only be opened from the other side.

The fix for George had to solve two separate problems, not one, because they
only look alike: first, he needed to stop treating ancient evidence as
current — so now he only weighs each tag's most RECENT results, a rolling
count rather than the entire lifetime record, so a tag that's rarely used but
genuinely good is never punished just for being infrequent. Second, even a
FRESH small sample shouldn't be trusted as if it were rock-solid proof — so
a thin sample now gets pulled gently toward the overall average until it has
enough evidence of its own to stand on, instead of being taken completely at
face value the moment it clears a minimum count.

Both changes were checked directly against George's own real self-test
checklist, and then against Kev's real historical numbers — the required
reward-to-risk did come down for the worst-affected tags, honestly and
measurably, though not by some dramatic amount overnight, because you can
only get so much out of reshaping old evidence — real improvement now needs
actual new trades to start closing under everything that's already been
fixed, so the Examiner finally has something current to remember.


WHERE THINGS STAND NOW
================================================================================

Four real problems, found one at a time, each one only visible once the
previous one had been dealt with:

  1. Dexter's own bar had already been lowered on purpose — a deliberate
     choice, working as intended.
  2. Elliot's separate grading had never been told about that choice, and was
     needlessly harsh on top of it — fixed.
  3. Chev's own detailed reasoning was being thrown away before anyone could
     actually read it — fixed.
  4. George was judging tonight's trades using yesterday's report card — the
     real headline discovery, and the fix that's most likely to actually
     matter.

Nobody in this story turned out to be broken on purpose, or lazy, or
careless. Every single piece was doing exactly what it was built to do. The
problem was never any one character — it was that a few of them had quietly
stopped listening to each other, and it took a careful, patient walk through
the whole building, one room at a time, to notice.
