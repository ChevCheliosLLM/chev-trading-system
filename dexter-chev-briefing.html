Inside the Chev Chelios trading system

What Dexter tells Chev — the analyst → the trader

Every time a setup is worth a look, Dexter writes Chev one long briefing — everything it noticed about the market — and Chev has to read the whole thing and decide what to do. Here's exactly what's in that briefing, where every number actually comes from, and what happens after Chev makes his call.

🛰️

Dexter

The scanner. Watches every market non-stop, never gets tired, never has a gut feeling — only numbers.

01Live price & recent candlesThe current price, plus a stretch of price history▸
Where the number comes from

Pulled live, right now, from Binance's price feed for crypto pairs (other feeds handle forex and stocks). Chev sees the actual recent candles for the timeframe in question, plus a couple of neighbouring timeframes for context — not a huge historical archive, just enough to see the recent story.

02Trend & momentum indicatorsEMA, RSI, ATR, VWAP, Bollinger Bands, MACD▸
Where the number comes from

Standard, well-known charting-industry formulas — the same ones any charting app uses — run directly on the price history Dexter just pulled. Nothing exotic: is price trending up or down, is it "overbought," how jumpy has it been lately.

03Support & resistance, per timeframeSeparate levels for the 15-min, 30-min, 1-hour and 4-hour charts▸
Where the number comes from

Dexter counts how many times price has bounced off roughly the same level, on each timeframe's own history. A level that's been touched several times gets flagged as more reliable than one that's only been touched once or twice — and levels that hold up across several timeframes at once are flagged as the strongest kind.

04Fibonacci levelsPullback levels from the latest real price swing▸
Where the number comes from

Dexter finds the most recent genuine high-to-low (or low-to-high) swing in price, then works out the standard Fibonacci percentages between those two points. It's anchored to an actual move that happened, not picked arbitrarily.

05Volume profileWhere the heaviest trading actually happened, price-wise▸
Where the number comes from

Dexter adds up how much volume traded at every price level during a detected move, then finds the single price with the most activity (the "point of control") and the price range that contains the bulk of all the volume.

06Order book snapshot (crypto only)Real buy/sell orders sitting on the exchange right now▸
Where the number comes from

Pulled live, directly from the exchange's own order book — including unusually large stacked orders ("walls") and thin gaps where price could move fast with little resistance.

07Futures positioning (crypto only)Are traders leaning long or short right now?▸
Where the number comes from

Pulled from Binance's futures market data — the funding rate (a signal for whether longs or shorts are "too crowded") and how much money is currently sitting in open futures positions.

08Market regimeTrending up, trending down, ranging, or just noisy chop▸
Where the number comes from

A standard trend-strength measurement run on the 4-hour chart. Dexter attaches a plain-English note to whatever it finds — e.g. "you're trading with the trend" or "this is counter-trend, hold yourself to a much higher bar."

09The structural readDexter's own deeper "story" of the market▸
Where the number comes from

Dexter's own custom logic looks for meaningful turning points in price, the moves between them, and builds a running hypothesis of what price is likely to do next — a second, independent opinion that gets checked against Dexter's simpler confluence read for agreement.

10Executable geometryThe real minimum stop, reward ratio, and target — before Chev picks numbers▸
Where the number comes from

Calculated straight from the account's actual trading costs (fees, slippage) and risk rules — not a guess. Chev is shown the real floor up front, so he isn't left guessing a number only to be told afterward that it wasn't good enough.

11Where the idea dies, and where it winsSpecific invalidation and target prices▸
Where the number comes from

Worked out from the size of the recent price move, the nearest real structural levels, and the account's own minimum-reward rules — a concrete "this proves the idea wrong" price, and a concrete "this is a genuine win" price.

12Housekeeping & contextOpen trades, past losses, playbook rules, news events▸
Where the number comes from

Pulled from the account's own trade history and a live economic calendar: Chev's currently open positions (so he doesn't stack similar risk twice), a warning if a very similar setup lost money recently, his own written trading rulebook, a few of his own recent trades for reference, which trading session is active, and any major news events coming up.

ONE MESSAGE, EVERY TIME
🎙️

Chev

The trader. Reads the whole case file, has a personality, makes the call — but doesn't get the last word.

What he actually receives

The exact same long report on the left, delivered as one message — plus his own separate rulebook that never changes: his personality, his trading philosophy, the things he's banned from doing, and the precise format he has to answer in.

How he processes it

He reads it like a written case file, not a spreadsheet. He writes out his reasoning in plain language first, then commits to specifics in a fixed format: direction, entry, stop, target, and which of the clues above (his "tags") actually convinced him.

"Confluence at the 0.618 level, RSI showing hidden bullish divergence, and I'm with the 4H trend — I'm taking this long."

Can he do his own math?

Yes — but only with what's already in front of him. He can work out his own risk/reward ratio from the entry, stop and target he just proposed, or compare his own read against Dexter's structural engine and note whether they agree. What he can't do is go fetch new data himself — everything he reasons about was already handed to him in that one message.

Does he get the final word?

No — and this is the important part. After Chev replies, a set of automatic checks re-verify everything: do his numbers match the real live price (catching anything that looks made up), does the trade clear the account's actual risk rules, does it structurally hold up.

🛡️ Any one of those checks can block the trade even after Chev says "post it." Chev proposes — the system's own math has the actual veto power.
